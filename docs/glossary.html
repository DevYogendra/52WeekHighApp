52-Week High Concepts

52-Week High (All-Time High, ATH)

Definition:

The highest price a stock has traded at during the past 52 weeks (one year).

Significance: Stocks hitting new highs show the strongest business momentum and investor conviction. It's a powerful filtering criterion.

Example:

TCS' 52W high is ₹4,500. It's trading at ₹4,400 today (-0.2%). This is very near the high, showing strength.

% from 52W High

Formula:

(Current Price - 52W High) / 52W High × 100

What it means: How far below its peak the stock is currently trading. Negative number = discount from high.

Range Interpretation
-0 to -3% At peak, maximum strength, momentum fresh
-3 to -10% Near high, still strong, pullback starting
-10 to -30% Pullback zone, value opportunity
-50%+ Deep value, requires research, risky

Momentum

Definition:

The tendency for a stock's price to continue moving in the same direction. Stocks that recently hit highs tend to continue up.

In this app: Measured by Trend Score, Δ Hits, and frequency metrics.

Why it matters: Momentum is a real market phenomenon. Stocks hitting new highs continue up 60-70% of the time for 4-12 weeks.

Δ Hits (Delta Hits)

Formula:

Δ Hits = Number of highs this week - Number of highs last week

What it means: Week-over-week change in high frequency. Shows if momentum is accelerating or decelerating.

Value Signal
+3 or higher Accelerating momentum, strong buy
+1 to +2 Growing momentum, positive
0 to -1 Stable to slightly declining
-2 or lower Decelerating momentum, caution

Trend Score

Formula:

Trend Score = (Hits in past 7 days × 3) + (Hits in past 30 days × 2) + (Hits in past 60 days × 1)

What it measures: Composite momentum score emphasizing recent activity. Higher = stronger momentum.

Why weighted this way: Recent highs are more important than old ones. 7-day activity gets 3x weight.

Score Momentum Strength
100+ Very strong, stock on fire
60-100 Strong, established trend
30-60 Moderate, emerging trend
<30 Weak momentum

📊 Valuation Metrics - How Expensive Is It?

Valuation metrics answer: "Is this stock overpriced or underpriced?" Lower usually means cheaper, but context matters.

P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)

Formula:

P/E = Stock Price ÷ Earnings Per Share (EPS)

What it means: How many years of current earnings you're paying for the stock.

Example:

Stock A: Price ₹100, EPS ₹10 → P/E = 10 (paying 10 years of earnings)

Stock B: Price ₹100, EPS ₹2 → P/E = 50 (paying 50 years of earnings)

Stock A looks cheaper (lower P/E), but A might be declining and B growing 40%/year. Context matters!

P/E Range Valuation Assessment
<10 Very Cheap
10-15 Cheap to Fair
15-25 Fair to Expensive
25-40 Expensive (growth priced in)
40+ Very Expensive (high risk)

P/BV (Price-to-Book Value)

Formula:

P/BV = Market Price ÷ Book Value Per Share

What it means: How much you're paying for each rupee of assets (after subtracting liabilities).

Who cares: Banks and asset-heavy companies (this matters). Tech companies (less important).

P/BV Range Meaning
<1.0 Trading below book value (distressed or cheap)
1.0-2.0 Fair value for assets
2.0-4.0 Premium for quality/growth
4.0+ Very expensive (growth priced in heavy)

PEG Ratio (Price-to-Earnings Growth)

Formula:

PEG = P/E Ratio ÷ Expected Annual Earnings Growth Rate (%)

What it means: P/E adjusted for growth rate. "Is this expensive for the growth I'm getting?"

Example:

Stock A: P/E 20, expected growth 20% → PEG 1.0 (FAIR VALUE)

Stock B: P/E 20, expected growth 10% → PEG 2.0 (EXPENSIVE)

Same P/E, but Stock B is more expensive because growth is slower.

PEG Value Assessment
<0.8 Excellent value for growth
0.8-1.2 Fair value for growth
1.2-1.8 Slightly expensive
1.8+ Expensive for growth

Earnings Yield

Formula:

Earnings Yield = (1 ÷ P/E Ratio) × 100%

What it means: Inverse of P/E. Annual return you'd get if all earnings went to you. Compare to FD rate!

Example:

Stock with P/E 20 → Earnings Yield 5%

Compare: Bank FD rate is 6-7%. Is 5% return worth the risk? Think about it.

💪 Quality Metrics - How Good Is The Business?

Quality metrics measure profitability and efficiency. Higher ROE = better quality company.

ROE % (Return on Equity)

Formula:

ROE = (Net Income ÷ Shareholders' Equity) × 100%

What it means: How much profit the company generates per rupee of shareholder money invested.

Why it matters: THE single most important quality metric. High ROE = efficient, profitable business.

Example:

Company A: ₹100 shareholder equity, ₹20 annual profit → ROE 20% (excellent)

Company B: ₹100 shareholder equity, ₹5 annual profit → ROE 5% (poor)

Company A is 4x more efficient at generating returns.

ROE % Quality Rating
20%+ Exceptional (Moat, best-in-class)
15-20% Excellent (Very high quality)
12-15% Good (Solid company)
8-12% Average (Acceptable)
<8% Poor (Low quality)

ROA % (Return on Assets)

Formula:

ROA = (Net Income ÷ Total Assets) × 100%

What it means: Profit generated per rupee of total assets. Shows asset efficiency.

Similar to ROE but:** ROA measures efficiency of ALL assets. ROE measures efficiency for equity holders.

ROA % Assessment
10%+ Excellent asset efficiency
5-10% Good asset efficiency
2-5% Average asset efficiency
<2% Poor asset efficiency

OPM % (Operating Profit Margin)

Formula:

OPM = (Operating Profit ÷ Total Sales) × 100%

What it means: How much profit the company makes from core operations, before interest/taxes.

Why it matters: Shows pricing power and operational efficiency. Higher = stronger competitive position.

OPM % Quality Rating
15%+ Excellent (Strong pricing power)
10-15% Good
5-10% Average
<5% Poor (Competitive pressure)

📈 Momentum Indicators - Is Money Flowing In?

Δ Gain % (Delta Gain Percentage)

Formula:

Δ Gain % = (Gain % this week - Gain % last week)

What it means: Week-over-week change in the stock's year-to-date gain. Shows acceleration/deceleration in price momentum.

Signal: Positive Δ Gain % = accelerating (good). Negative = decelerating (caution).

DII/FII Flows (Institutional Buying/Selling)

Components:

DII: Domestic Institutional Investors (Indian mutual funds, insurance, pension funds)

FII: Foreign Institutional Investors (foreign funds, foreign portfolio investors)

What it means: Net buying or selling by smart money. Positive = buying. Negative = selling.

Flow Scenario Signal
Both DII + FII positive Very bullish: All smart money buying
DII positive, FII neutral Positive: Domestic buying
One positive, one negative Mixed: Unclear direction
Both DII + FII negative Very bearish: Smart money exiting

Market Cap Change %

Formula:

Δ% MCap = (This week's market cap - Last week's market cap) / Last week's market cap × 100

What it means: Week-over-week change in total market value. Shows if money is flowing into company.

Δ MCap % Meaning
+5%+ Strong money inflow
+2% to +5% Healthy inflow
-5%+ Strong money outflow

⚖️ Balance Sheet Health - Is The Company Safe?

Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E)

Formula:

D/E = Total Debt ÷ Total Equity

What it means: How much the company borrows vs. owns. Higher D/E = more leveraged = riskier.

Example:

Company A: ₹50 debt, ₹100 equity → D/E 0.5 (safe)

Company B: ₹200 debt, ₹100 equity → D/E 2.0 (risky, high leverage)

Company A has safer balance sheet.

D/E Ratio Safety Assessment
<0.3 Very safe (low debt)
0.3-0.7 Safe (healthy debt)
0.7-1.0 Moderate (acceptable)
1.0-1.5 High (risky)
>2.0 Very high (dangerous)

Always check: Before buying any stock, verify D/E < 1.0. This single check saves you from leverage disasters.

Current Ratio (Liquidity)

Formula:

Current Ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities

What it means: Can the company pay its bills in the next 12 months? Shows short-term financial health.

Current Ratio Assessment
1.5+ Strong liquidity
1.0-1.5 Adequate liquidity
<1.0 Weak liquidity (risky)

💧 Liquidity & Efficiency Metrics

Inventory Turnover

Formula:

Inventory Turnover = Cost of Goods Sold ÷ Average Inventory

What it means: How many times per year the company sells and replaces inventory.

Higher is better: Inventory moves fast → cash flow efficient → less stuck capital.

Receivables Turnover

What it means: How quickly the company converts sales into cash. Higher = faster cash collection.

Red flag: Declining receivables turnover = customers delaying payments = cash flow deteriorating.

Working Capital

Formula:

Working Capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities

What it means: Cash available to run day-to-day operations. Higher = better positioned.

Red flag: Negative working capital = company struggles to pay short-term bills.

5 Complete Investment Strategies

1️⃣ Momentum Strategy

Philosophy: Chase recent winners hitting new highs with accelerating gains and institutional buying.

Time Horizon: 2-8 weeks (swing trades)

Key Metrics to Monitor:

Entry Signals

✓ Δ Hits +2 or higher
✓ Trend Score 40-60
✓ DII/FII positive

Exit Signals

✗ Δ Hits turns -2 or lower
✗ Trend Score >100 (overbought)
✗ DII/FII both turn negative

Position Size: 1-2% per trade (higher risk)

Risk Management: Stop loss at -10% of entry price. Use tight stops because momentum can reverse quickly.

2️⃣ Quality Growth Strategy

Philosophy: Find companies with strong fundamentals (ROE 15%+, low debt) hitting new highs = double winners.

Time Horizon: 6-24 months (buy & hold)

Key Metrics to Screen:

Buying Criteria

✓ ROE 15%+
✓ D/E < 0.7
✓ P/E 12-25
✓ Trend Score 30+

Hold/Sell Triggers

Sell if: ROE drops 5%+ or D/E climbs >1.0 or P/E jumps >40

Position Size: 2-3% per stock (lower risk, best for wealth building)

Why it works: ROE 15%+ compounds to 10x wealth over 10 years. Quality at discounts creates outsized returns.

3️⃣ Value + Momentum Hybrid

Philosophy: Combine momentum signals with quality checks = find winners that are also financially healthy.

Time Horizon: 3-12 months (medium-term)

The Setup:

Find

Stock with ROE 12%+, D/E <0.8 showing positive Δ Hits

Buy When

Pullback + momentum returns = best risk/reward ratio

Position Size: 2-3% per stock

Best Risk/Reward: Downside protected by quality, upside captured by momentum.

4️⃣ Deep Value Contrarian

Philosophy: Find severely beaten-down companies (50%+ off highs) with decent fundamentals that will bounce back.

Time Horizon: 2-5 years (patient capital required)

Critical Criteria:

Entry Requirements

✓ 50-70% below 52-week high
✓ ROE still 8%+
✓ D/E < 1.5
✓ Thesis confirmed

Avoid

✗ Negative ROE
✗ D/E > 2.0
✗ No clear recovery story

Position Size: 0.2-0.5% max per stock (high risk). Scale in on further lows.

Exit Strategy: Take 25-50% profits on first bounce, hold rest for 3+ years for 5-10x returns.

5️⃣ Sector Rotation

Philosophy: Follow sector trends. Rotate portfolio capital into hot sectors, out of cold ones.

Time Horizon: 4-week rebalancing cycles (tactical)

The System:

Weekly Check

View 10: Which sectors have 10+ new highs? THOSE are hot.

Action

30-50% portfolio in hot sector stocks. Rebalance monthly.

Position Size: 2-5% per stock, 30-50% per hot sector

Why it works: Sector momentum is structural. IT in bull = 40% portfolio in INFY/TCS. Auto in bear = near 0%.
